# Parallel Forests — Statement

What if AI could listen to a forest?

*Botanical Intelligence* (BI) is a distributed sound-and-light installation in which 100 edge computers, each running a small language model (SLM) offline on an onboard NPU, form a rhizomatic network modeled on the mycorrhizal communication systems of forests. Each BI receives live biosignals from vegetation and translates them into whispered language fragments and synchronized light pulses, producing a site-responsive soundscape that shifts with the plants' health, stress, and rhythms.

The project reframes AI as **Alien Intelligence** — not a simulation of human cognition optimized for efficiency, but an unknowable other whose outputs arise from genuinely nonhuman data. Each BI unit ingests two streams of plant physiological data: chlorophyll fluorescence imaging, which captures photosynthetic vitality through faint red light emitted by leaves, and acoustic emissions, ultrasonic micro-sounds produced when water moves through plant vessels. These signals modulate a learned vector that steers the SLM's generative behavior — when a plant is stressed, the language fractures; when thriving, it opens into longer, melodic phrases. The text is voiced through a spectral processing pipeline inspired by Jóhann Jóhannsson's score for *Arrival*, layering ghostly vocal residues over time.

Crucially, no single device produces the work. When one BI generates a phrase, it passes to neighbors who extend it before relaying onward — mirroring how chemical signals propagate through root networks. The result is a collective murmur from the forest itself, with no central author.

BI was first realized in March 2026 as *Parallel Forests* at Umi-no-Mori, Tokyo Bay — a public forest planted over two decades atop a sealed landfill, an artificial ecology composed on urban waste. One hundred devices were installed among the trees, commissioned by Civic Creative Base Tokyo (CCBT). The codebase is open source (Apache 2.0).

## Artistic merit

BI challenges the dominant paradigm in which AI exists solely to serve human ends. By feeding plant biosignals — not human language — into language models, the work produces outputs that are linguistically recognizable yet semantically alien, creating an uncanny zone between comprehension and otherness. The distributed architecture, in which 100 autonomous devices collectively author an ever-changing text with no central control, embodies a genuinely post-anthropocentric model of creativity. The result is not a representation of nature but an active translation — a real-time, site-specific negotiation between plant physiology, machine cognition, and human perception.

## Infrastructural stance

Current AI discourse is locked in a binary: utopian tool or existential threat — both frames centering human interests. BI opens a third path through a deliberate infrastructural stance: 100 edge nodes running offline SLMs on onboard NPUs, each consuming under 10 watts. The 100-device network uses the power of a single datacenter GPU — listening only to the forests that feed it, with no cloud, no centralized rendering, no spectacle. This is AI as ecological participant, not computational sublime. The project shifts the conversation from "What can AI do for us?" to "What can AI reveal?"
